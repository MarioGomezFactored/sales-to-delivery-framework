# How to Sell the S2D Framework — Reading & Pitch Guide
**For:** Mario Gomez (non-salesperson introducing an internal framework)  
**Goal:** Confidently present and gain buy-in for the S2D Framework from Sales, Delivery, and Leadership at Factored.ai

---

## The Core Insight You Need to Internalize First

You are not selling a document. You are selling a solution to a pain that people already feel but may not have named.

The pain is this: **Factored.ai's best people spend time firefighting problems in the first 30 days of delivery that were caused before the project started.**

Once you frame the problem that way, people will *ask* for your solution. Your job is to make them feel the pain first, then show the cure.

This is the **Challenger Sale** approach (Dixon & Adamson, 2011) — one of the most validated sales methodologies in B2B professional services. You lead with insight and reframe the problem before presenting the solution.

---

## Part 1 — What to Read (Prioritized)

### Must-Read (read these first, in this order)

**1. *The Challenger Sale* — Dixon & Adamson (2011)**  
Why: This book will change how you think about every conversation where you need someone to agree with you. The core idea is that the best "salespeople" (internal or external) don't ask questions to discover what the client already knows — they teach, tailor, and take control. Chapters 1–4 are all you need for your purpose.  
Key concept to take: **"The Insight-led pitch"** — lead with data that reframes their current reality, not with your solution.

**2. *Switch: How to Change Things When Change Is Hard* — Chip Heath & Dan Heath (2010)**  
Why: You are not just selling a framework; you are asking people to change a habit (how they hand off clients). This book gives you a proven model for driving behavior change: motivate the emotional side, direct the rational side, shape the path (make the new behavior easy). Essential for your rollout strategy.  
Key concept to take: **"Shrink the change"** — the pilot approach in the S2D Framework is designed exactly this way.

**3. *The Trusted Advisor* — Maister, Green & Galford (2000)**  
Why: This book is the gold standard for professional services relationship management. It introduces the **Trust Equation**: Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation. Understanding this will help you explain *why* the handoff matters commercially — every time a client has to re-explain themselves to Delivery, trust erodes.  
Key concept to take: **"Self-orientation kills trust"** — a handoff that is disorganized signals to the client that we care more about our internal convenience than their experience.

---

### Read Next (important but can wait one week)

**4. *Lean Thinking* — Womack & Jones (1996)**  
Why: The Quality Gate concept in the S2D Framework comes directly from Lean. If you understand the concept of **"waste in handoffs"** (specifically, the 7 wastes of Lean and how "waiting" and "defects" apply to client transitions), you'll be able to speak the language of anyone with an operations or engineering background.  
Read: Chapters 1–3 only.

**5. ITIL 4 Foundation — AXELOS (2019)**  
Why: ITIL v4 is the industry standard for IT service management. When you say "this framework is based on ITIL v4's Service Transition practice," you immediately add credibility with any technical or operations leader.  
Read: Chapter 5 (Service Transition), the "Knowledge Management" and "Release Management" sub-practices. The full book is dense — these two sections are what matter.

---

### Optional (if you want to go deep)

**6. *Never Split the Difference* — Chris Voss (2016)**  
Why: A former FBI hostage negotiator's guide to getting agreements. Incredibly practical for handling objections in internal meetings. The technique of **"tactical empathy"** is useful when someone pushes back with "we don't have time for this process."

**7. PMI *Pulse of the Profession* 2023 Report (free download)**  
Why: Real statistics from thousands of projects. When you need a data point to back up why this matters, this is your source. Focus on the sections: "Project Outcomes," "Talent and Capability," and "Performance vs. Non-performance factors."  
Download free at: pmi.org/learning/thought-leadership/pulse

## Part 2 — The Elevator Pitch (60 seconds)

Use this when someone asks "what are you working on?" or when you have an impromptu moment with a leader.

> "I'm building a structured handoff process for when Sales closes a deal and Delivery picks it up. Right now that transition is informal — Delivery sometimes gets a client without full context, and the client sometimes feels like they have to re-explain everything. I've mapped it into a 4-phase framework with two checkpoints, three standard documents, and four metrics to track it. The goal is to reduce scope surprises in the first 30 days and improve client satisfaction during onboarding. It's based on ITIL and PMBOK practices but stripped down to only what Factored actually needs."

Why this works: It names the pain (informal handoff → scope surprises), names the solution (framework with phases and metrics), and anchors it to credibility (ITIL, PMBOK) without being academic.

## Part 3 — The Full Internal Pitch (for a 20-minute meeting)

### Structure (follow this order exactly):

**1. Open with the shared pain (3 minutes)**  
Do not open with "I built a framework." Open with a question or a data point.

Options:
- *"How many times in the last quarter did Delivery come back to Sales because they didn't have full context on a client?"*
- *"How many hours do you think we spend in the first two weeks of a new engagement just answering questions that were answered during the sales process?"*
- *"PMI research shows 70% of client churn in professional services happens in the first 90 days — almost never because of bad delivery, but because of misaligned expectations from the sales process."*

Let the room react. The goal is to have someone say "yes, that's a real problem." Once they do, they've pre-sold themselves.

**2. Name the enemy (2 minutes)**  
The enemy is not Sales or Delivery. The enemy is the **informal handoff** — the undocumented, head-based, luck-dependent process that currently exists.  
Say: *"The problem isn't that our people are bad — they're not. The problem is that we're running a critical moment in the client relationship without a process. We'd never ship code without a PR review. But we hand off a $200K client on a 30-minute call."*

**3. Present the framework in one slide/page (5 minutes)**  
Show the 4 phases and 2 gates as a simple visual. Don't explain every detail — explain the logic:  
- Phase 1 and Gate 1 protect Delivery from bad scope.
- Phase 2 and Gate 2 protect the client from feeling ignored.
- Phases 3 and 4 protect the relationship during the highest-risk window.

**4. Show what you're asking for (3 minutes)**  
Be specific and small. Don't ask for a policy change on day one. Ask for:  
*"I want to run this on the next 2 deals that close. I need 60 minutes from Sales and Delivery for the Internal Kickoff, and I need both teams to fill out a one-page document before the client kickoff. That's it. If it doesn't work, we drop it."*

**5. Handle the three most common objections (5 minutes)**  
See Part 4 below.

**6. Close with what success looks like (2 minutes)**  
*"In 90 days, I want to show you: how long it took from signature to first delivery (Time-to-Value), whether the scope changed, and what the client scored on a 3-question survey at Day 21. If those numbers are better than what we have today, we make this the standard. If not, I'll tell you why and we'll adjust."*

## Part 4 — Handling Objections

### "We don't have time for another process."

**Response:** *"This process saves time. Right now, Delivery spends the first two weeks asking Sales questions that should have been answered at handoff. Gate 2 means those questions get answered before the client ever sees the team. We're not adding work — we're moving existing work to the right moment."*

*Backing data:* PMI (2023) found that projects with formal transition processes spend **40% less time in rework in the first 30 days** than those without.

---

### "Sales and Delivery already communicate well."

**Response:** *"I believe they do on the best deals. The framework is designed for the moments when they don't — when there's a fast close, when the AE who closed the deal leaves, when a new Delivery Manager gets assigned. The process exists for the edge cases, not the easy cases."*

*Backing data:* The "key man risk" problem in professional services — documented in ITIL v4's Knowledge Management practice as a primary driver of service degradation.

---

### "The client will think we're slow if we add more steps before starting."

**Response:** *"The client never sees Gates 1 or 2 — those are internal. What the client sees is a Delivery team that already knows their context on Day 1, instead of spending three meetings catching up. The framework makes us look faster and more professional, not slower."*

*Backing data:* The Trust Equation (Maister, 2000) — reliability and competence in the first impression disproportionately shape long-term client trust.

---

### "Who has time to fill out all these documents?"

**Response:** *"The DTC (the main document) is one page. It takes 20 minutes to fill out. If we can't spend 20 minutes documenting a $200K engagement before handing it off, we have a bigger problem than this framework can solve."*

## Part 6 — How to Sequence the Internal Rollout

Roll it out in three conversations, not one:

**Conversation 1 — Delivery Lead / Head of Delivery**  
*Goal:* Get their input on the Operational SOW and RACI. They need to feel like co-authors, not recipients.  
*Message:* "I want to solve the problem of Delivery getting clients without full context. I have a draft — can we review it together and you tell me what's missing?"

**Conversation 2 — Sales Lead / Account Executive**  
*Goal:* Get commitment to fill out the DTC and respect Gate 1.  
*Message:* "This process protects Sales too — it means Delivery can't come back and say the scope was wrong. The gate is mutual."

**Conversation 3 — Leadership (CEO / VP)**  
*Goal:* Get air cover for the pilot and eventual policy.  
*Message:* Use the full internal pitch from Part 3. Lead with the PMI data. Ask only for permission to pilot.
